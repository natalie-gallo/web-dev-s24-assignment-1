The Arctic
Arctic Fox

The Arctic Fox (Vulpes lagopus) is a resilient and adaptive species native to the Arctic tundra. Known for its iconic white fur that camouflages with the snowy landscape, the fox undergoes a seasonal color change, turning brown during the summer months. This small carnivore is well-adapted to the harsh Arctic environment, with a keen sense of hearing and a thick, insulating coat that helps it survive in frigid temperatures. Arctic foxes are opportunistic hunters, scavenging for small mammals and birds, and are known for their playful behavior, especially during the breeding season.

Arctic Hare

The Arctic Hare (Lepus arcticus) is a hardy herbivore inhabiting the Arctic tundra and other cold regions. Renowned for its white coat in winter, the hare molts to a gray or brown hue during the summer, providing effective camouflage against predators. Large hind legs enable swift movement across the snow-covered terrain, and its well-insulated fur provides protection against the extreme cold. Arctic hares are primarily herbivores, consuming a variety of plant materials, and they employ keen senses to detect predators in the vast and open Arctic landscape.

Walrus

The Walrus (Odobenus rosmarus) is a massive marine mammal thriving in the Arctic and sub-Arctic regions. Recognizable by its long tusks and distinctive whiskers, the walrus is well-adapted for life in both icy waters and on sea ice. These social creatures are often found in large herds, and they rely on a diet of benthic invertebrates, using their sensitive whiskers to locate prey on the ocean floor. Walruses are known for their impressive haul-out sites, where they rest, socialize, and give birth. Climate change and diminishing sea ice pose challenges to their habitat and survival.

Snowy Owl

The Snowy Owl (Bubo scandiacus) is an iconic bird of prey inhabiting the Arctic tundra and surrounding regions. Recognized for its striking white plumage, the snowy owl is superbly adapted to its snowy surroundings. With keen eyesight and exceptional hunting skills, it preys on lemmings and other small mammals. Unlike many owl species, snowy owls are diurnal, active during both day and night. Their large size and distinctive appearance make them a symbol of the Arctic ecosystem, and they undertake impressive long-distance migrations in search of prey and suitable nesting sites.
